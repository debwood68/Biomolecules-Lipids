Section 01
What Are Lipids?

Lipids differ from other biological macromolecules in an important way: they are not defined by a shared chemical structure or a monomer-polymer relationship. Instead, they are grouped together because they share a physical property — they are hydrophobic (water-fearing) or amphipathic (having both hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions). This hydrophobicity arises from their predominantly hydrocarbon composition. Ch. 5, p. 75

The major types of lipids are fats (triglycerides), phospholipids, steroids, and waxes. Each has a distinct structure and function, but all share the defining characteristic of being largely or entirely non-polar.

🔑 Key Distinction Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, lipids are not true polymers — they are not assembled from repeating monomers by dehydration synthesis in the same systematic way. A triglyceride is assembled from glycerol and three fatty acids, but these are not "monomers" in the polymer sense. This is why lipids are sometimes called a "diverse group" rather than a macromolecule family in the strict sense. Ch. 5, p. 75
Section 02
Fats — Triglycerides

A fat (triglyceride) consists of one glycerol molecule joined to three fatty acid chains by ester bonds, formed via dehydration synthesis — one water molecule is released per bond, so three water molecules are released in total. The glycerol backbone is a three-carbon alcohol; each fatty acid is a long hydrocarbon chain with a carboxyl group (—COOH) at one end. Fig. 5.10, p. 76

Section 03
Saturated vs Unsaturated Fatty Acids

The key structural variable in fatty acids is whether the hydrocarbon chain contains any carbon-carbon double bonds (C=C). A saturated fatty acid has no double bonds — every carbon is bonded to the maximum number of hydrogens. An unsaturated fatty acid has one or more C=C double bonds, introducing a kink in the chain. pp. 76–77

Saturated vs Unsaturated Fatty Acid Chains
Figure 2 — Saturated fatty acids have straight chains that pack tightly together, producing solid fats (e.g. butter, lard). Unsaturated fatty acids have kinked chains that prevent tight packing, producing liquid oils (e.g. olive oil). (Campbell 12th ed., Fig. 5.11, p. 77)

⚠ Trans Fats Natural unsaturated fatty acids have a cis configuration at the double bond — the hydrogen atoms on the same side, producing the characteristic kink. Industrial hydrogenation creates trans fats, where hydrogens are on opposite sides, straightening the chain. Trans fats behave more like saturated fats structurally and are strongly associated with cardiovascular disease. Many countries have now banned or heavily restricted them. p. 77
Section 04
Phospholipids & the Cell Membrane

A phospholipid has a similar backbone to a triglyceride — glycerol with fatty acid chains — but with a critical difference: one fatty acid is replaced by a phosphate group linked to a small polar molecule (such as choline). This gives the phospholipid two distinct regions: a hydrophilic head (the charged phosphate group) and two hydrophobic tails (the fatty acid chains). A molecule with both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions is described as amphipathic. Fig. 5.12, p. 78

Phospholipid Structure & Bilayer Assembly
Figure 3 — A phospholipid is amphipathic: its polar head is hydrophilic and its two fatty acid tails are hydrophobic. In water, phospholipids spontaneously form bilayers with heads facing outward into aqueous environments and tails sequestered in the hydrophobic interior. (Campbell 12th ed., Fig. 5.12, p. 78 & Fig. 7.2, p. 128)

This amphipathic structure means phospholipids spontaneously organise into bilayers in aqueous environments — the hydrophobic tails shield themselves from water by facing inward, while the hydrophilic heads face the aqueous environment on each side. No energy input is required. This is the structural basis of all biological membranes. pp. 78, 128

Section 05
Steroids & Waxes
🔷
Steroids
Lipids with a characteristic four-ring carbon skeleton. Cholesterol is the most important steroid in animals — it is a component of cell membranes (where it modulates fluidity) and is the precursor for all steroid hormones (testosterone, oestrogen, cortisol) and bile acids. Despite its reputation, cholesterol is essential for life at normal concentrations.
Fig. 5.14, p. 80
🕯️
Waxes
Long-chain fatty acids joined to long-chain alcohols by ester bonds. Highly hydrophobic and very stable. Form waterproof coatings on plant leaves (cuticle), insect exoskeletons, bird feathers, and mammalian fur. Beeswax is a familiar example. Their extreme hydrophobicity makes them excellent barriers to water loss.
p. 80
🧱
Cholesterol in membranes
Cholesterol molecules sit within the phospholipid bilayer, with their polar hydroxyl group near the phosphate heads and their hydrophobic rings among the fatty acid tails. At warm temperatures, cholesterol restrains phospholipid movement (reducing fluidity); at cold temperatures, it prevents tails from packing too rigidly (maintaining fluidity).
p. 132
⚡
Fat as Energy Storage
Triglycerides store more than twice as much energy per gram as carbohydrates (~9 kcal/g vs ~4 kcal/g) because their hydrocarbon chains are highly reduced. They are also anhydrous — stored without water — making them a far more efficient energy store by mass than glycogen, which is stored with water.
p. 76
Section 06
Key Terms — Lipids
Lipid
A diverse group of hydrophobic or amphipathic molecules unified by their insolubility in water. Includes fats, phospholipids, steroids, and waxes.
Triglyceride
A fat molecule consisting of glycerol joined to three fatty acid chains by ester bonds formed via dehydration synthesis. Primary long-term energy storage molecule.
Fatty acid
A long hydrocarbon chain with a carboxyl group (—COOH) at one end. Can be saturated (no C=C) or unsaturated (one or more C=C, introducing kinks).
Ester bond
The covalent bond linking a fatty acid to glycerol in a fat or phospholipid. Formed by dehydration synthesis between the carboxyl group of the fatty acid and a hydroxyl group of glycerol.
Saturated fat
A fat containing only saturated fatty acids (no C=C bonds). Straight chains allow tight packing; solid at room temperature. Found mainly in animal products.
Unsaturated fat
A fat containing one or more unsaturated fatty acids (one or more C=C bonds). Kinked chains prevent tight packing; liquid at room temperature. Found mainly in plants and fish.
Phospholipid
A lipid with a glycerol backbone, two fatty acid tails, and a phosphate-containing head group. Amphipathic — the structural basis of all biological membranes.
Amphipathic
Having both a hydrophilic region and a hydrophobic region. Phospholipids are amphipathic, which drives their spontaneous self-assembly into bilayers.
Steroid
A lipid with a four-ring carbon skeleton. Cholesterol is the most important animal steroid — a membrane component and precursor for steroid hormones.
Hydrophobic
Water-fearing — non-polar molecules that do not interact favourably with water. Hydrocarbon chains are hydrophobic because they have no polar bonds to form hydrogen bonds with water.

Section 08
Check Your Understanding

Answer these questions to test your understanding before the class session.

Question 1 of 5
Why are lipids classified together as a group, despite their structural diversity?
✓ Correct. Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids — which are defined by their shared monomer-polymer chemistry — lipids are defined by a shared physical property: hydrophobicity. This is why triglycerides, phospholipids, steroids, and waxes are all called lipids despite having very different structures. (Ch. 5, p. 75)
✗ The answer is C. Lipids have no common monomer. Not all contain glycerol (steroids and waxes do not). The unifying feature is their hydrophobic or amphipathic nature — they are insoluble in water because they are made predominantly of non-polar hydrocarbon.
Question 2 of 5
Olive oil is liquid at room temperature while butter is solid. What structural difference between their fatty acids explains this?
✓ Correct. C=C double bonds in unsaturated fatty acids introduce kinks that prevent adjacent chains from packing closely. Without tight packing, intermolecular forces are weaker and less energy (heat) is needed to overcome them — so the oil remains liquid at room temperature. Saturated chains in butter align tightly, requiring more energy to disrupt — hence solid at room temperature. (pp. 76–77, Fig. 5.11)
✗ The answer is B. Both olive oil and butter are triglycerides built from glycerol and three fatty acids. The difference is in the fatty acids: unsaturated (C=C double bonds, kinked chains) in olive oil vs saturated (no double bonds, straight chains) in butter. Kinks prevent tight packing → weaker intermolecular forces → lower melting point → liquid at room temperature.
Question 3 of 5
A phospholipid is described as amphipathic. What does this mean, and why is this property essential for membrane formation?
✓ Correct. Amphipathic means having both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions. When placed in water, phospholipids spontaneously arrange into bilayers: the hydrophilic heads interact with the aqueous environment on each side, while the hydrophobic tails hide from water by facing each other in the interior. No energy input is required — this self-assembly is thermodynamically spontaneous. (Fig. 5.12, p. 78)
✗ Amphipathic means having both a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and hydrophobic (water-fearing) tails. In water, the hydrophobic tails spontaneously arrange to minimise contact with water — which drives bilayer formation. The heads face outward into the aqueous environment and the tails face inward. This requires no energy input.
Question 4 of 5
Fat stores approximately 9 kcal/g of energy, while glycogen stores approximately 4 kcal/g — and glycogen is also stored alongside water, roughly tripling its effective mass. What does this tell us about why animals store long-term energy as fat rather than as glycogen?
✓ Correct. For an animal that needs to move, carrying unnecessary mass has a real energetic cost. Fat's high energy density (~9 kcal/g, stored anhydrously) vs glycogen's lower density (~4 kcal/g, stored hydrated) means fat stores roughly 6× more energy per gram of actual body mass. A human carrying their entire energy reserve as glycogen would weigh many times more than they do. (p. 76)
✗ The answer is D. The key is energy efficiency by mass. Fat stores over twice the energy per gram compared to carbohydrates, AND it is stored dry (anhydrous) while glycogen is stored with water (adding mass without adding energy). For a mobile animal, this makes fat a vastly more efficient energy store.
Question 5 of 5
Cholesterol is often described negatively in popular culture. Based on what you have read, what are the essential biological roles of cholesterol that make it indispensable?
✓ Correct. Cholesterol has two essential roles: (1) membrane fluidity buffer — at high temperatures it slows phospholipid movement; at low temperatures it prevents solidification; (2) precursor molecule — all steroid hormones (testosterone, oestrogen, cortisol, aldosterone) and bile acids are synthesised from cholesterol. Problems arise from excess cholesterol, particularly oxidised LDL-cholesterol deposits in arterial walls — not from the molecule's normal roles. (pp. 80, 132)
✗ The answer is C. Cholesterol does not replace phospholipids — it sits among them in the bilayer, modulating their movement. It is also the essential precursor for steroid hormones and bile acids. At normal concentrations it is indispensable; health problems arise from elevated plasma cholesterol, particularly LDL-cholesterol, not from cholesterol's normal cellular functions.
